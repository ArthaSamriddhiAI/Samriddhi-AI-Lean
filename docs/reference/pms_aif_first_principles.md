# First Principles: Why PMS/AIF, and When Not

*This section sits before Part I. Everything that follows in this document assumes you have read it and accepted its premise. If you disagree with the premise, the rest of the document will feel like it is solving the wrong problem.*

---

## The Starting Position

The default recommendation for any Indian HNI should be a low-cost, diversified portfolio built from index funds and well-chosen active mutual funds. Total cost: 0.3 to 1.0 percent per year. Complexity: minimal. Liquidity: daily. Tax efficiency: reasonable. Regulatory protection: maximum (SEBI MF regulations, the most mature and investor-protective framework in Indian securities law). Behavioural protection: high (pooled vehicle insulates the investor from their own worst impulses).

This is not a radical position. It is the position that the data supports.

PMS and AIF are departures from this default. Every departure must justify itself with a specific, articulable, data-supported reason that the default cannot satisfy. An advisor who cannot state, in plain language, why a specific PMS or AIF is better for a specific client than the default portfolio has no business recommending it. The product's existence is not a justification. The product's marketing is not a justification. The advisor's trail commission is not a justification. Only the client's measurable, demonstrable benefit is a justification.

This is the starting position of this document, and it should be the starting position of any honest PMS/AIF advisory practice.

## What the Data Actually Shows

Before any investment thesis, the numbers.

**PMS performance reality (from the 513-fund dataset, as of February 2026):**

The median 5-year post-fee alpha across 240 PMS funds with sufficient track record is **+0.81 percent per year** over their self-selected benchmarks. The mean is +1.74 percent, pulled up by a right tail of genuinely exceptional managers. Roughly **42 percent of funds (101 of 240) delivered negative 5-year alpha** against their own stated benchmark, after fees. These are not obscure funds. They are SEBI-registered, professionally managed, marketed to sophisticated investors, and they underperformed a benchmark you could have bought for 0.1 percent per year in an index ETF.

By category, alpha is concentrated where markets are least efficient. Small-cap PMS: median 5Y alpha +3.6 percent. Small-and-mid-cap: +3.1 percent. Multi-cap/flexi-cap (the largest category, 268 of 513 funds): +0.5 percent. Large-cap: **minus 0.9 percent**. The median large-cap PMS not only fails to beat its benchmark, it loses to it. After fees.

183 of 513 funds (36 percent) are younger than three years. They have never been tested in a bear market. Their track records are artifacts of the market environment in which they were born, not evidence of skill.

Survivorship bias makes all of these numbers optimistic. The funds that failed, wound down, or were merged into other strategies after underperforming are not in the dataset. The actual experience of an investor who picked a random PMS five years ago is worse than what the surviving-fund statistics show.

**AIF performance reality:**

Harder to measure because most Cat I and Cat II AIFs are closed-ended with unrealised holdings, and reported NAVs are estimates, not market prices. But the structural facts are clear: management fees of 2 percent on committed capital (not deployed, creating an effective rate of 5 to 12 percent on deployed capital in early years), performance fees of 20 percent above hurdle, and for Cat III, a fund-level tax drag of 25 to 35 percent on gross gains. An AIF must generate substantial gross alpha merely to break even against the default after all costs.

**The honest summary:** PMS/AIF, as a category, does not reliably outperform simple, cheap alternatives for the median investor. It outperforms for a minority of funds with genuine, persistent, cycle-tested skill. Identifying those funds ex ante is the core skill of PMS/AIF advisory, and most advisors are not as good at it as they believe.

## The Four Genuine Theses

Given the data above, when is PMS/AIF genuinely justified? There are four theses. Each applies to a specific client situation. None of them is "PMS/AIF exists, therefore my client should be in it."

### Thesis 1: The Mutual Fund Envelope Has Become a Constraint

A mutual fund manager running ₹20,000 crore cannot take a 10 percent position in a mid-cap stock without moving the market. A mutual fund cannot hold unlisted equity. It cannot short securities. It is boxed in by SEBI diversification norms, concentration limits, and the structural requirement to meet daily redemptions with cash. These constraints protect retail investors. They also cap the return available to sophisticated investors whose capital is patient enough to tolerate concentration and illiquidity.

PMS escapes these constraints. A PMS manager holding 15 to 20 stocks with conviction, unconstrained by index weight, unrequired to hold cash for daily redemptions, and able to personalise the mandate (sector exclusions, tax-loss harvesting timed to the client's fiscal year) has a structural advantage over a mutual fund running the same strategy at 50x the AUM.

**When this thesis holds:** The client has ₹50 lakhs or more to allocate to a single equity strategy. The manager has a demonstrable, cycle-tested track record of generating net-of-fee alpha in the specific market segment (small/mid-cap, thematic, concentrated). The strategy operates in a segment where mutual fund constraints are binding (not large-cap, where mutual funds have no structural disadvantage). The client's tax situation benefits from pass-through treatment and the ability to time gain/loss realisation.

**When this thesis fails:** The client is being offered a large-cap PMS. The data says the median large-cap PMS underperforms its benchmark after fees. The structural advantage of PMS over mutual funds is smallest in large-cap, because large-cap stocks are deeply liquid, widely followed, and efficiently priced. A Nifty 50 index fund at 0.1 percent TER is almost certainly the better choice. The thesis also fails when the manager's alpha has not survived a bear cycle, or when the fee structure (2.5 percent fixed with no performance alignment) is extractive rather than aligned.

### Thesis 2: The Client Needs Access to Non-Public-Market Asset Classes

Growth-stage private equity. Venture capital. Structured corporate credit at 14 percent yield. Pre-IPO equity. Real estate debt. Infrastructure yield. These asset classes genuinely do not exist in the public market. A mutual fund cannot invest in them. A direct investor lacks the sourcing, underwriting, and portfolio-construction expertise. Cat I and Cat II AIFs are the regulated vehicle for accessing these return streams.

The diversification argument is real. Listed Indian equity, across all its permutations (large-cap, mid-cap, small-cap, sector, thematic), is still one asset class in one country. It moves together in crises. A portfolio that is 100 percent listed Indian equity, however cleverly diversified within that envelope, is fully exposed to a single-country, single-asset-class risk. Cat II PE, with its 0.3 to 0.5 correlation to listed markets and its 1 to 2 quarter valuation lag, provides genuine diversification. Cat I VC, with its binary outcomes and 7 to 10 year horizon, provides genuine access to the growth premium of unlisted companies. Cat II structured credit, with its 12 to 18 percent yield and credit-risk-not-market-risk profile, provides genuine income diversification.

**When this thesis holds:** The client has ₹1 crore or more of genuinely surplus capital (capital that will not be needed for any purpose, under any scenario, for 5 to 10 years). The client's existing portfolio is concentrated in listed assets and would benefit from genuine (not cosmetic) diversification. The GP has a credible track record in the specific strategy, a co-investment that aligns incentives, and a fee structure that does not consume the diversification premium. The client understands and accepts the J-curve, the capital-call mechanics, and the impossibility of early exit.

**When this thesis fails:** The client has ₹1 crore to invest but it represents 30 percent of their liquid net worth. Locking 30 percent of liquid capital for 7 years is not diversification; it is a liquidity trap. The thesis also fails when the GP is unproven (first fund, no prior track record in the strategy), when the fee structure is aggressive (American waterfall with no clawback), or when the client is being sold "PE" as a prestige product rather than as a portfolio-construction tool. Wanting to tell friends at the club that you invest in private equity is not an investment thesis.

### Thesis 3: The Client Has a Specific Risk-Management Need That Long-Only Cannot Address

Most HNI wealth in India is correlated. The business is in India. The property is in India. The salary (if salaried) is earned in India. The social capital, political risk, and regulatory exposure are all India-denominated. Adding more long-only Indian equity to this mix does not reduce risk; it amplifies it.

A genuinely market-neutral strategy (net zero beta, returns uncorrelated to the Nifty) addresses this. In a 2008-style crisis where the client's business revenue drops 30 percent, their property value drops 20 percent, and the Nifty drops 50 percent, a market-neutral allocation holds its value (or generates positive returns) precisely when everything else is falling. That is not a return story; it is an insurance story. The "return" on a market-neutral strategy is not the absolute number; it is what that number does to the client's total-wealth volatility and their ability to survive a systemic event without forced selling.

**When this thesis holds:** The client has ₹10 crore or more of total wealth with heavy India/equity concentration. The client has a specific, articulable hedging need (business risk, career risk, property risk, ESOP concentration). The strategy is genuinely market-neutral, not "long-short that is actually 70 percent net long" (which is what most SIF and many Cat III strategies are). The client understands and accepts the fee-and-tax drag (which for Cat III is 25 to 35 percent of gross gains at fund level) as the cost of the hedge, not as an expectation of net alpha.

**When this thesis fails:** The client is being sold a Cat III AIF as a return-generating product. The pitch is "18 percent gross returns." After the 2+20 fee structure and fund-level tax, the net is 8.5 to 10 percent. A Nifty 500 index fund would have delivered 13 to 15 percent net over the same period. Unless the client specifically needs the hedging property, the Cat III is a worse deal. The thesis also fails below ₹10 crore of total wealth, because at lower wealth levels the fee-and-tax overhead of Cat III consumes too large a fraction of the total portfolio for the hedging benefit to be meaningful.

### Thesis 4: The Client Needs Customisation That Pooled Vehicles Cannot Provide

This is the narrowest thesis and the most defensible. A mutual fund holds the same portfolio for every investor. A PMS can hold a different portfolio for each client.

Specific customisation needs that only PMS can address: a business owner who needs to exclude their own sector from the equity portfolio (a pharma manufacturer who should not hold pharma stocks, because their business income and their portfolio are then correlated). An investor going through a succession event who needs specific securities held in specific names. An investor with a complex tax situation who needs gain/loss realisation timed to specific months. An investor with ESG or religious constraints that standard fund mandates do not accommodate.

**When this thesis holds:** The customisation need is real, specific, and documented. The PMS mandate explicitly reflects the customisation. The advisor has verified that no mutual fund or SIF achieves the same outcome (increasingly, MFs offer sector-exclusion options and direct-plan customisation that narrow this gap).

**When this thesis fails:** The "customisation" is generic ("I want a portfolio tailored to me") rather than specific. Generic customisation is marketing language for "the same portfolio everyone else gets, but with your name on the statement." True customisation has a testable, verifiable feature that the pooled alternative lacks.

## The Decision Tree

Before recommending any PMS or AIF, the advisor should walk this tree. The Samriddhi E6 agent should enforce it.

**Step 1: Can the client's stated need be met by a mutual fund, index fund, or SIF?**

If yes: recommend the simpler product. Stop. The advisor earns less trail. The client gets a better net outcome. That is the job.

If no: proceed to Step 2. Document exactly why the simpler product fails. "Client needs sector exclusion for pharma" or "Client needs exposure to pre-IPO equity" or "Client needs genuinely market-neutral hedging" are valid reasons. "Client wants higher returns" is not a valid reason, because the data says the median PMS/AIF does not deliver higher net returns than the default.

**Step 2: Does the client meet the structural prerequisites for PMS/AIF?**

For PMS: ₹50 lakhs of genuinely investable capital (not capital needed for near-term obligations). Ability to absorb a 30 to 40 percent drawdown without forced selling. Understanding of direct-ownership mechanics and tax implications. No Layer 3 constraints (significant debt, personal guarantees, active litigation) that make concentrated equity exposure inappropriate.

For AIF: ₹1 crore of genuinely surplus capital (the "under no scenario, for any reason, for 5 to 10 years" test). Liquid assets elsewhere sufficient to cover all life events, business contingencies, and capital calls from existing commitments during a simultaneous market drawdown. Understanding and acceptance of illiquidity, J-curve, and capital-call mechanics. No Layer 3 constraints that make lockup inappropriate.

If prerequisites are not met: do not recommend. Document why. Suggest a timeline for revisiting ("once your personal guarantee exposure drops below ₹3 crore, AIF becomes viable").

If prerequisites are met: proceed to Step 3.

**Step 3: For the specific product proposed, does the net-of-all-costs expected return exceed the default alternative by a margin that justifies the added complexity, fee load, and (for AIFs) illiquidity?**

Compute three numbers:
1. The product's expected net-of-fee, net-of-tax return, using the client's specific tax bracket and the product's historical return and fee data.
2. The default alternative's expected net-of-fee, net-of-tax return, using the same tax bracket and the appropriate benchmark (Nifty 500 for multi-cap PMS, Nifty Smallcap 250 for small-cap PMS, a blended equity-plus-debt benchmark for Cat II PE, etc.).
3. The delta: product minus default.

If the delta is not positive by a margin that exceeds estimation uncertainty (roughly 1.5 to 2.0 percentage points per year for PMS, 3 to 4 percentage points per year for illiquid AIFs): do not recommend on return grounds. The product may still be justified on Thesis 3 (hedging) or Thesis 4 (customisation) grounds, but the advisor must articulate the non-return justification explicitly.

If the delta is positive and significant: recommend. Document the specific thesis (1, 2, 3, or 4) that applies, the specific client pattern that makes the thesis relevant, and the specific expected net benefit in rupees over the investment horizon.

**Step 4: Present the counterfactual to the client.**

"I am recommending [Product X]. Here is what I expect it to deliver net of all costs over [Y] years: approximately [₹Z]. Here is what the simpler alternative would deliver over the same period: approximately [₹W]. The difference is [₹Z minus ₹W], which represents the value I believe this product adds for you. The costs of this additional value are: [specific costs: fee drag, illiquidity, complexity, tax inefficiency if Cat III]. I believe the benefits outweigh the costs because [specific thesis]. Do you agree?"

No product should be placed without this conversation. The advisor who cannot have this conversation honestly does not understand the product well enough to recommend it.

## What This Means for the Rest of This Document

Everything from Part I onward is written for the advisor who has passed through this decision tree and concluded that PMS/AIF is the right frame for a specific client. The document teaches you how to evaluate products, construct portfolios, manage relationships, and navigate the operational mechanics of PMS/AIF. It does not repeatedly re-litigate whether PMS/AIF is the right answer, because you have already answered that question here.

But the question was answered here, not assumed away. And if at any point during the advisory process the answer changes (the client's situation shifts, the product underperforms, the thesis that justified the original recommendation no longer holds), the advisor's obligation is to revisit this section, re-run the decision tree, and if the answer is now "no," to recommend the simpler alternative, even at the cost of their own trail income.

That is the first principle. Everything else is execution.
